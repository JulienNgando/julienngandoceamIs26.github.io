what_i_learned: |
  Today’s research focused on mapping out the architectural evolution of deep learning models, specifically comparing Feedforward Neural Networks (FNNs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) alongside their advanced variants, LSTMs and GRUs. I deep-dived into how basic FNNs handle static data vectors, whereas CNNs excel at capturing spatial hierarchies using localized receptive fields and shared weights—critical features for handling grid-like spatial data. Transitioning from spatial to sequential data, I analyzed standard RNNs, which introduce temporal loops to retain information from previous time steps but suffer heavily from the vanishing gradient problem when handling long-term dependencies.

  To understand how modern frameworks overcome this temporal limitation, I explored Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs). LSTMs elegantly manage the flow of historical context through a dedicated cell state regulated by three specialized mechanisms: input, forget, and output gates. In contrast, the GRU offers a streamlined alternative by merging the cell and hidden states, using only reset and update gates to control information flow. Evaluating these trade-offs is essential for determining when the lighter computational footprint of a GRU is preferable over the robust memory capacity of a full LSTM when modeling complex, time-varying phenomena.
blockers: |
  Faced some conceptual friction when diving into the exact mathematical mechanics of the backpropagation through time (BPTT) algorithm for LSTMs. Isolating exactly how the error gradient propagates through the cell state over long sequences without decaying requires a lot of meticulous matrix calculus tracking, which stalled momentum midday.
reflection: |
  It is fascinating to see how deep learning architectures are fundamentally specialized toolsets rather than one-size-fits-all solutions. A model's structural design dictates how it perceives data—whether as a static snapshot, a spatial grid, or a continuous temporal stream. Moving forward, the goal is to shift from studying these frameworks in isolation to strategically leveraging their distinct architectural strengths for complex, multidimensional modeling challenges.
