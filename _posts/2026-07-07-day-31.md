what_i_learned: |
  Today brought a major breakthrough in understanding our model's behavior, though it came in the form of diagnosing a critical flaw in our pipeline. While reviewing our preprocessing steps, I realized we had inadvertently introduced data leakage by lagging and rolling our target variable. Because the rolling window incorporated future information relative to the specific training time steps, the model was essentially "cheating" during training, leading to artificially inflated performance metrics and severe overfitting. It explained why our validation scores looked too good to be true, and it was a stark reminder of how easily subtle data dependencies can compromise a machine learning pipeline.

  After isolating and stripping out the contaminated features to restore structural integrity to our data split, I shifted focus to the visualization pipeline. I went back and refactored the code responsible for generating our modeling $R^2$ performance graphs. The updated scripts now correctly reflect the true, un-leaked predictive metrics of our models across training and testing phases, ensuring our final evaluations are both mathematically sound and transparently documented. 
blockers: |
  Untangling the feature engineering script to locate exactly where the target leakage leaked into the training set took significant debugging time. Figuring out how to properly align the pandas index for the rolling calculations without shifting future target data backwards required a deep dive into window function constraints. Additionally, rewriting the visualization code to dynamically update the $R^2$ plots with the corrected model outputs without breaking the existing layout added an extra layer of frustration.
reflection: |
  Finding out your model is overfitting due to data leakage is always a bit humbling, but I am incredibly glad we caught it now rather than later. It underscores the absolute necessity of rigorous data hygiene, especially when working with time-dependent features where temporal boundaries are easily blurred. Resolving this issue means our baseline metrics are going to drop to realistic levels, but building a robust, honest model is infinitely more valuable than chasing flawless, artificial validation scores.
