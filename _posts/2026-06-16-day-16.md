what_i_learned: >
  Today was highly technical and focused heavily on data granularity and feature engineering. I started with my baseline dataset, which was structured in hourly intervals, and evaluated the predictive accuracy of several machine learning models. To capture more detailed temporal dynamics, I resampled and split the hourly data into 30-minute intervals, subsequently comparing how the change in granularity impacted the performance metrics across the different models under evaluation.

  Additionally, we covered advanced time-series machine learning techniques, specifically lagging and rolling windows. I implemented these features into my pipeline to provide the models with historical context, allowing them to better learn sequential dependencies and trends within the data.
blockers: >
  The biggest blocker was ensuring the technical accuracy and stability of the model outputs. At several points during the feature engineering and interval splitting phases, the models produced mathematically unreasonable predictions. This required digging back into the data pipeline, identifying structural anomalies, and performing rigorous code fine-tuning to stabilize the results.
reflection: >
  Transitioning from static, hourly evaluations to dynamic, high-frequency time-series modeling underscored how sensitive machine learning algorithms are to feature design. Implementing lagging and rolling techniques highlighted the balance between giving a model enough historical context and introducing noise or overfitting. Encountering and debugging the erratic predictions was a valuable reminder that data preparation and iterative code refinement are just as critical to research success as the underlying theory itself.
