what_i_learned: >
  Today at my research group meeting, I learned about the distinct characteristics of Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD) and PM2.5. While PM2.5 measures the physical mass of fine particulate matter near the ground, AOD tracks how these particles absorb or scatter light throughout the entire atmospheric column. I also explored various satellite retrieval algorithms used to measure AOD, including NASA's prominent Dark Target algorithm. Exploring these different concepts gave me a much stronger foundation in the environmental data modeling relevant to our team.

  Following the meeting, I completed Google Research's AI walkthrough to bridge the gap between this data and machine learning. The platform allowed me to run mock simulations on data collection and AI model training, which provided a brilliant, practical insight into my upcoming research workflows. To experiment further with large language models, I also built a few quick chatbot applications using Groq today. Gaining hands-on experience with these fast APIs has made me much more aware of how modern AI architectures function under the hood.
blockers: >
  The only blockers I had today were on the machine learning side. When building quick apps with tools like Groq, I hit initial roadblocks with API connection errors, but eventually, I squared everything out.
reflection: >
  Looking back at today’s work, it is clear that successful AI research requires a strong bridge between the data itself and the machine learning models that process it. Diving into atmospheric physics showed me just how messy raw scientific data can be—whether it is trying to reconcile ground-level PM2.5 with column-wide AOD, or dealing with data gaps in satellite algorithms like Dark Target. Understanding these data limitations up front is essential because an AI model is only as good as the information we feed it.

  Stepping through the Google Research simulations and building quick applications with Groq helped ground these complex concepts into practical engineering skills. Running mock workflows made my upcoming research goals feel much more tangible, while working directly with LLM APIs gave me a clearer intuition for how modern AI handles data under the hood.
