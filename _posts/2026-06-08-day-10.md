what_i_learned: >
  Today, I transitioned into hands-on data handling by working with multiple datasets that required merging and restructuring. My first task was to combine the initial datasets and convert the data into hourly intervals to establish a consistent time frame. From there, I calculated the average of the Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD) data so that it could be properly integrated with my meteorological data.
  
  To successfully merge the AOD and meteorological datasets, I had to align them by identifying exact timestamp matches. I achieved this by holding the AOD statistics constant and checking for identical dates and times across both sets. Once the merger was complete, I cleaned the dataset by removing any missing values. Finally, I printed the shape of the data frame to inspect the final row and column counts, allowing me to verify exactly how many data points were added or dropped during the process.
blockers: >
  Today, I finally dove into the deep end of hands-on data handling, which turned out to be a masterclass in troubleshooting. My main objective was to merge multiple datasets and convert them into hourly intervals, but I immediately hit a wall with mismatched timestamp formats and indexing errors that took some time to untangle. Once the time series was aligned, I calculated the average of the Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD) data to merge it with my meteorological data—a process that required holding the AOD statistics constant while hunting for exact date and time matches. This part was particularly tedious, as a few missing data chunks threw off the alignment, forcing me to carefully drop null values without compromising the integrity of the remaining data. Finally, printing the dataset's shape to see the final row and column counts felt incredibly satisfying, giving me a clear picture of exactly what was dropped and confirming that the data was finally clean and ready.
reflection: >
  Looking back at how this project started, I can feel a massive shift in my confidence as a programmer. In the beginning, the sheer volume of fragmented data and the constant threat of syntax errors felt incredibly daunting. But navigating today’s blockers—debugging mismatched timestamp formats, handling missing values, and successfully merging complex datasets like AOD and meteorology—proved to me that I can actually do this. Instead of feeling paralyzed when an error pops up, I now view it as just another puzzle to solve. 
  
  This breakthrough has changed how I see coding entirely. It is no longer just an academic exercise or a series of isolated tutorials; it is a powerful tool I actually know how to wield. For the first time, I genuinely feel ready to transition from just fixing code to actively building things from scratch. Knowing that I can take raw, messy data and engineer it into a clean, usable structure gives me the foundation I need to start bringing my own project ideas to life.
