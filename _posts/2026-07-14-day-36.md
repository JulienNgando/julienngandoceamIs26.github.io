what_i_learned: |
  To push past the implementation phase and build a stronger mathematical foundation for our modeling, I dedicated a significant portion of today to reading a foundational paper on recurrent architectures. The paper went deep into how recurrent neural networks (RNNs) map sequential input vectors to hidden state representations over time, specifically highlighting the mathematical bottlenecks of standard backpropagation through time (BPTT). It was highly illuminating to see how researchers formally analyze the exploding and vanishing gradient problems, mapping out how the repeated multiplication of the recurrent weight matrix can cause gradients to either vanish to zero or grow exponentially over longer sequence lengths.

  Beyond just diagnosing the flaws of basic RNNs, the paper walked through the architectural innovations of gated mechanisms. Seeing the rigorous formal derivations for how LSTMs and GRUs use additive error carousels to maintain a constant gradient flow completely changed how I view our sliding window setup. Rather than just treating these networks as "black boxes" that ingest PM2.5 sequences, I now have a much clearer picture of how the update and reset gates dynamically scale their memory retention. This theoretical grounding is going to be incredibly useful as we begin fine-tuning our hyperparameter configurations for the sequential models.
blockers: |
  Parsing through the dense matrix calculus and eigenvalue proofs used to demonstrate the vanishing gradient problem in the paper was incredibly slow going. It took multiple read-throughs and some side-scratchpad math to fully bridge the gap between the paper's theoretical proofs and how those mathematical bottlenecks actually manifest in our PyTorch training loops.
reflection: |
  It is easy to get caught up in the "just make the code run" mindset when developing machine learning models, but taking the time to read academic literature is incredibly grounding. Understanding the underlying mathematics behind why an architecture behaves a certain way completely reframes how you troubleshoot performance issues. It makes you realize that hyperparameter tuning shouldn't just be a guessing game of trial and error, but a deliberate design choice based on how information propagates through the network.
