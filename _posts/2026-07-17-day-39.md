what_i_learned: |
  Today's research mixed deep data analysis with hands-on fieldwork. I started the day by diving back into our visualization pipeline to reconstruct and interpret our Mean Absolute Error (MAE) graphs for the $PM_{2.5}$ predictive models. Analyzing these plots allowed me to track exactly how the average prediction error scales across different forecasting horizons and time intervals. It was interesting to visualize how the MAE tightly correlates with our previous $R^2$ findings, showing a distinct drop in absolute error variance when transitioning from the 60-minute averages to the more granular 30-minute resampled intervals.

  The highlight of the day, however, was taking our data collection out of the lab and into the air. I actually got to fly a research drone equipped with onboard atmospheric sensors to measure $PM_{2.5}$ concentrations in real time. Watching the live data stream in as the drone shifted altitudes gave me a whole new perspective on the vertical distribution of particulate matter, providing a fascinating, real-world contrast to the static historical datasets we’ve been modeling all week.
blockers: |
  We ran into some unexpected calibration and telemetry sync errors right before launching the drone. The onboard particulate sensor was lagging when pairing with our ground control data logger, which forced us to spend a couple of hours troubleshooting wireless protocols and resetting the hardware scripts out in the field. 
reflection: |
  Bridging the gap between data science and physical data collection is incredibly rewarding. Analyzing MAE curves on a computer screen teaches you a lot about algorithmic precision, but piloting a drone and watching those $PM_{2.5}$ metrics fluctuate live in the atmosphere reminds you of the dynamic environment your models are actually trying to simulate. It really underscores the practical impact of our research.
