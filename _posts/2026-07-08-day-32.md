what_i_learned: |
  Today's research came with a major plot twist: it turns out yesterday's panic about data leakage and overfitting was a false alarm. After deep-diving back into the data pipeline, I discovered that the underlying logic of our target feature engineering was actually sound. However, there was a subtle indexing error specifically affecting our RNN model's performance score. When setting up the sliding windows to structure our sequential data, my code accidentally sliced too many of the PM2.5 columns, feeding redundant features into the network and skewing the evaluation metrics. 

  Once I isolated the indexing bug, I refactored the sliding window logic to ensure the RNN only consumes the precise history steps it's supposed to. With the data boundaries properly constrained, the model's actual performance score settled exactly where it should be—meaning our project baseline is completely intact and error-free. To celebrate closing that stressful loop, I spent the evening unwinding with my fellow co-workers for a fantastic game day and a movie night, which was the perfect way to cap off a hectic couple of days.
blockers: |
  Tracing the indexing mismatch across the multidimensional arrays used for the RNN's sliding windows was incredibly tedious. Because the shape of the tensor looked correct on the surface, it took a lot of manual print statements and step-by-step array inspection to find the exact line where the extra PM2.5 columns were sneaking into the feature matrix. 
reflection: |
  If this week taught me anything, it's that code hygiene and thorough validation are a double-edged sword. It is easy to assume the worst and assume a model is fundamentally broken or "cheating" when the metrics look strange, but sometimes the culprit is just a simple slice index in a pandas or numpy array. Catching this "false flag" reinforced the importance of thoroughly verifying an error before completely rewriting a pipeline—and it made the team bonding night afterward feel much more deserved!
